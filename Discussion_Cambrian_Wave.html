There is strong suggestion in the findings of this theory that life is a uniform cosmic process
throughout the universe. But what is meant by uniform? Well if my Planck constant for the solar
system applies to stars beyond our star system, then many G2 V main sequence stars like our
Sun (yellow-white color and temperature, hydrogen burning) may have habitable planets like
the Earth. The Planck constant for the solar system might be universal because it is here
connected to the proton in a very dynamic way. Uniform might mean five-fold symmetry that is
typical of life on Earth, for example five fingers on each hand, or two arms, two legs and a head,
or two eyes a nose, and a mouth on the face. And it may mean a genetic code like we have, DNA
and RNA, based on purines like adenine, guanine, cytosine, thymine, and uracil, with phosphate
backbones, and ribose sugar. Uniform might imply things like this, but our equations are not
biochemistry but planetary, which might be the conditions for such biology. Uniform here
means subtle variations on an idea, like here on Earth — variations in skin color, or tall/short,
variations in hair color, or four legs with fur, but variations in patterns like stripes or dots.
The idea of uniformity throughout the Universe in life may have begun with the British
astronomer Fred Hoyle. He suggested planets were seeded by one process with the components
for life to take hold and evolve. To do this he figured we needed carbon as universal to have life
as we have it here, based on carbon. Carbon seems to do the job because it has 4 valence
electrons allowing for the long chains of hydrocarbons involving, nitrogen, oxygen, phosphorus,
and sulfur predominantly. Silicon has four valence electrons as well but readily combines with
oxygen so easily to make SiO2 (silicon dioxide) the primary component of sand and glass, so it is
not available to combine with anything else. Hoyle then had to show how stars make carbon
from lighter elements, which he did in his triple alpha process.
So this is what is called astrobiology or exobiology, that considers not just how life is on Earth,
but how it might be throughout the Universe. There are now textbooks in this fairly new field,
and it is starting to be offered in astronomy departments at universities. With good reason
because though we cannot yet travel the immense distances to the stars, we have begun to be
able to explore the planets of our solar system. We are at that point, with computers and robots
becoming so sophisticated now, that we are able to do this.
