Abstract
This paper strives to address a question suggested by my theory that is whether life is part of a
cosmic process. We have a theory for the Earth/Moon/Sun system as analogous to a quantum
mechanical system for the atom. It uses a Planck constant for the Earth/Moon/Sun system and
it is related to the Planck constant, h, of quantum mechanics. It seems to produce Earth orbit
as fundamental in its connection to the atom. We have to ask then if, since the Earth is
connected to the atom as a physical system, does the theory apply to other star systems. If it
does it would suggest life is part of a cosmic process, and we have to ask if it is, what is
behind it. To answer whether life is part of a cosmic process, then, we have to see if what was
found applies to other star systems with life-bearing worlds. While the data is scant and
ballpark, we have some and are able to apply the formula to Kepler-452b which has a
terrestrial planet in the habitable zone of its star. The only problem is we currently can detect
some planets around stars, but we can not yet resolve something like a moon orbiting an
exoplanet because such a thing is far to small to resolve. We can, however, make an educated
guess as to what its mass might be given the size of the planet it orbits. The formula involves
the moon of a life-bearing world, and for a world to bear life it is believed it needs a moon to
hold its inclination to its orbital plane, relatively steady. It may be our formula only holds for
main sequence spectral-type G stars, like our Sun (or spectral types close to it). The majority of
exoplanets so far detectable with current technology are spectral class M, red dwarf stars,
more than likely because such stars are the most populous in our galaxy. The theory is in my
paper The Sixfold Unfolding of the Universe and Life, Ian Beardsley, 2023.

Red Dwarf Stars
It may be that our formula for habitable Earthlike planets (terrestrial, or solid planets, not gas
giants like Jupiter and Saturn) only applies to main sequence spectral class G stars, or spectral
classes near there, which is what the Sun is (right in the middle of the main sequence, average in
temperature). For instance Gliese 1002b orbits a M5V (red dwarf) star about 16 light years from
Earth. It is a possible habitable planet of Gliese 1002, has a mass of greater than or equal to 1.08
Earth masses and a radius of about 1.03 Earth radii. All good, it is much like Earth in these
respects, but it orbital period, year, is only 10.3 days. Its moon would have to be extremely small
for our equations to work, to produce 1 second. This is the story of many habitable planets in the
habitable zone for spectral class M (red dwarf stars). There are many such exoplanets like this
with short orbital periods, in fact they make up most of what we have been able to detect with
current technology, because red dwarf stars are the most populous in the galaxy. Most of their
planets in the habitable zone are closer in because these stars are not very hot, and thus have
short orbital periods, and are in fact tidally locked, that is their rotation has been slowed down
from gravity from the star to the point their rotation equals their orbital period so they always
have the same face facing the star as they orbit it, like the Moon is with the Earth. Such planets
would be very hot on their day side and very cold on their night side and the only place there
could be life on such a planet would be at the terminator, where day meets night, where it is
always twilight.
