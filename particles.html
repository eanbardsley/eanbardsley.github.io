Introduction
This collection brings together three related works that explore a single, striking numerical
pattern: the appearance of the human-defined second (1 s) in the fundamental properties of
elementary particles. The pattern first emerged in A Universal Particle Equation, where it was
shown that the proton, neutron, and electron each satisfy the relation
with and . This invariant leads to a universal particle equation
in which mass is interpreted as the resistance to rotating a particle's four-velocity from the time
direction into space. The equation successfully predicts the proton radius to within experimental
error and unifies the masses of the three stable particles around a common timescale.
However, the muon and tau leptons – with lifetimes many orders of magnitude shorter than one
second – do not satisfy the invariant. This raises natural questions: does the theory fail, or does it
simply have a domain of validity? The second paper in this collection, On Short-Lived Particles
and the 1-Second Invariant, addresses this question directly. It shows that the invariant
inherently assumes the particle exists long enough to complete a full phase cycle of the
temporal resistance field. Because muons and taus decay before that cycle completes, they lie
outside the theory’s regime. The paper then explores tentative, phenomenological modifications
(damping terms, coherence factors) as possible directions for extending the theory – while
making clear that these remain speculative and separate from the core result.
Finally, the third piece, Frequently Asked Questions: The 1-Second Invariant and the Universal
Particle Equation, clarifies common misconceptions. It answers questions such as: “Does the
electron need a full second to ‘acquire’ its mass?” (no – the invariant is a static ratio, not a
timer), “Is this just numerology?” (the proton radius prediction is testable and matches
experiment), and “Why do muons and taus not fit?” (their lifetimes are too short for coherence).
The FAQ serves as a companion for readers who wish to understand the theory’s assumptions,
limitations, and interpretation without wading through detailed derivations.
Taken together, these three papers present a coherent, if unconventional, framework: a 1-second
invariant rooted in the properties of stable particles, a clear boundary where the invariant fails
(short-lived particles), and a set of clarifying answers to anticipated questions. The collection is
