with \(\kappa_p = \kappa_n = 1/(3\alpha^2)\) and \(\kappa_e = 1\). This invariant leads to a universal particle equation

\[ m_i = \kappa_i \sqrt{\frac{\pi r_i^2 F_n}{G}}, \qquad F_n = \frac{h}{c\cdot(1\,\text{s})^2}, \]

in which mass is interpreted as the resistance to rotating a particle's four‑velocity from the time direction into space. The equation successfully predicts the proton radius to within experimental error and unifies the masses of the three stable particles around a common timescale.

However, the muon and tau leptons – with lifetimes many orders of magnitude shorter than one second – do not satisfy the invariant. This raises natural questions: does the theory fail, or does it simply have a domain of validity? The second paper in this collection, On Short‑Lived Particles and the 1‑Second Invariant, addresses this question directly. It shows that the invariant inherently assumes the particle exists long enough to complete a full \(2\pi\) phase cycle of the temporal resistance field. Because muons and taus decay before that cycle completes, they lie outside the theory’s regime. The paper then explores tentative, phenomenological modifications (damping terms, coherence factors) as possible directions for extending the theory – while making clear that these remain speculative and separate from the core result.

Finally, the third piece, Frequently Asked Questions: The 1‑Second Invariant and the Universal Particle Equation, clarifies common misconceptions. It answers questions such as: “Does the electron need a full second to ‘acquire’ its mass?” (no – the invariant is a static ratio, not a timer), “Is this just numerology?” (the proton radius prediction is testable and matches experiment), and “Why do muons and taus not fit?” (their lifetimes are too short for coherence). The FAQ serves as a companion for readers who wish to understand the theory’s assumptions, limitations, and interpretation without wading through detailed derivations.

Taken together, these three papers present a coherent, if unconventional, framework: a 1‑second invariant rooted in the properties of stable particles, a clear boundary where the invariant fails (short‑lived particles), and a set of clarifying answers to anticipated questions. The collection is intended for readers open to novel connections between quantum mechanics, gravity, and the nature of time – whether as a serious proposal or as a provocative stimulus for further theoretical work.
