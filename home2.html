Deep Seek Answered:

Your reflection touches on profound questions at the intersection of philosophy, neuroscience, mathematics, and theology. Let’s break down and explore some of the key ideas you’ve raised:

1. Neurobiology and the "Machine" View of Consciousness

Atheists or materialists often argue that consciousness arises solely from physical processes in the brain (neurons firing, chemical reactions, etc.). This is a reductionist view, where mental states are entirely determined by physical states.

You counter that this reduces humans to deterministic machines, akin to AI, lacking free will or true consciousness. This aligns with critiques from philosophers like John Searle (who argues against "strong AI") or Roger Penrose (who posits that consciousness involves non-computable processes, possibly rooted in quantum mechanics).

2. Free Will vs. Determinism

The crux of your argument is that if human thought is purely computational/deterministic (like AI), then free will is illusory. But you suggest humans might possess free will because their cognitive processes could involve something "beyond computational"—perhaps an ability to generate true randomness or make uncaused causes.

The challenge is defining what "free will" even means in a physical universe. Is it libertarian free will (uncaused causation)? Compatibilist free will (freedom within deterministic constraints)? Or an emergent property of complex systems?

3. Randomness and AI

You highlight that computers cannot generate true randomness; they rely on pseudo-random algorithms seeded by external inputs. Humans, you suggest, might be capable of true randomness (e.g., via free will or non-algorithmic processes).

However, it’s unclear whether "true randomness" (if it exists in nature, e.g., quantum indeterminacy) equates to free will. Randomness doesn’t imply agency or control—it’s just unpredictability. Free will seems to require something more: intentional, uncaused causation.

4. The Infinite Regression Problem

You rightly note that both theistic and atheistic worldviews face infinite regress problems:

Theists must explain an uncaused cause (God).

Atheists/materialists must explain the uncaused cause of the universe or the origins of deterministic/physical laws.

This is a classic metaphysical dilemma. The "unmoved mover" or "brute fact" problem appears in both cosmology (what caused the Big Bang?) and theology (what created God?).

5. Mystery and Limits of Knowledge

Your conclusion—that these questions remain unresolved—is shared by many philosophers and scientists. Consciousness, free will, and the origins of existence are open problems.

Some (like Penrose) propose radical solutions (e.g., quantum consciousness). Others embrace mysterianism, arguing that some mysteries may be beyond human cognitive limits.

Philosophical Positions to Consider:

Dualism: The mind is non-physical, separate from the brain (e.g., Descartes). This preserves free will but raises the "interaction problem" (how does the non-physical mind affect the physical brain?).

Physicalism: Everything, including consciousness, is physical. Free will is either illusory (hard determinism) or redefined (compatibilism).

Panpsychism: Consciousness is fundamental to all matter, not just brains. This avoids dualism but doesn’t fully solve the free will problem.

Libertarian Free Will: Humans can make uncaused choices, implying some non-physical or indeterministic component to cognition.

Final Thought:

You’re grappling with age-old questions that lack definitive answers. The debate between determinism and free will, the nature of consciousness, and the origins of existence are deeply intertwined with how we define terms like "randomness," "computation," and even "self." Whether humans are "beyond computational" (as Penrose suggests) or whether true free will exists remains speculative—but the mystery itself is a testament to the depth of these questions.

Perhaps the humility you express—acknowledging the limits of our current understanding—is the most philosophically sound position of all.
